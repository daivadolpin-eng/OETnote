REPARATION / RENOVATION
rep-uh-RAY-shun | ren-uh-VAY-shun 4 syllables each
British (RP): /ˌrepəˈreɪʃən/ | /ˌrenəˈveɪʃən/

Greg noted "renovation" is perhaps more natural when describing building repairs. Derek used this when describing his home water damage story in the warmup.

DEFICIT vs DEFICIENCY
DEF-i-sit | di-FISH-en-see 3 + 4 syllables
British (RP): /ˈdefɪsɪt/ | /dɪˈfɪʃənsi/
🎵 Deficit: HIGH-low-low — peak on "DEF" (short /e/). Deficiency: low-HIGH-low-low — peak on "FISH" (short /ɪ/, soft /ʃ/). Greg correction: Derek said "she has a deficit of vitamin D" → should be "a vitamin D deficiency".
⚠️ Greg's rule: deficit = strictly "not enough of something" (narrower). Deficiency = "not enough" OR "not working/absorbing properly" (the bigger umbrella word). Use deficiency when discussing vitamins, minerals, and enzyme conditions — it covers both shortage and absorption failure.
Deficit — specific shortage
An attention deficit means the person does not have enough sustained focus — it is a specific shortage of a cognitive resource.
Deficiency — broader
Iron deficiency can result from poor dietary intake or impaired absorption — it is a broader clinical concept than simply not enough iron.
📌 Greg's distinction:
deficit = not enough (shortage only)  |  deficiency = not enough OR not working properly (broader umbrella)

LESSON 16
EMBARRASSED vs EMBARRASSING
em-BARE-ist | em-BARE-i-sing 3 + 4 syllables
British (RP): /ɪmˈbærɪst/ | /ɪmˈbærɪsɪŋ/
🎵 Both: peak on "BARE" (short /æ/). Greg correction from class: "I felt embarrassing" → "I felt embarrassed". The -ed form describes how you feel; the -ing form describes what causes the feeling.
⚠️ Greg's rule: embarrassed (-ed) = how YOU feel (the person experiencing it). Embarrassing (-ing) = what CAUSES the feeling (the situation or event). Same rule applies to: tired/tiring, bored/boring, confused/confusing, exhausted/exhausting. After "I felt / I was", always use the -ed form.
Greg's correction — Derek's actual mistake
❌ "I felt embarrassing after the mistake." → ✅ "I felt embarrassed after the mistake."
-ing form used correctly
It was an embarrassing situation — but he handled it professionally.
📌 Same -ed / -ing distinction — person vs situation:
tired (you feel it) / tiring (it causes it)  |  bored / boring  |  confused / confusing
LESSON 16
MODAL VERBS IN NATURAL SPEECH: "must-b" / "my-b" / "carn-be" / "must-uv"
must-b | my-b | KARN-be | must-uv linked forms
must be /mʌsb/ | might be /maɪb/ | can't be /kɑːnbi/ | must have /mʌsəv/
🎵 All four: the content word (must / might / can't) carries the pitch peak. The following "be" or "have" reduces almost entirely — a quick, light tail. Greg's key point: Asian students stress every syllable equally — this sounds robotic. Cut and link naturally.
⚠️ Greg's core pronunciation note for this lesson: in natural speech, modal + be/have links and reduces. The T in must and might is cut or softened. "Have" reduces to "-uv". These forms don't sound like two separate words — they link into one fluid unit. The meaning remains clear from context.
Natural linked forms — from dictation sentences
"House thinks the answer must-b something simple." / "One doctor says it my-b a rare heart infection." / "It carn-be just one disease." / "She must-uv carried the virus on her hands."
OET speaking application
"This must-b a medication interaction — the timing fits perfectly with when she started the new drug."
📌 Greg's certainty scale — choose by how sure you are:
must be / must have (~90% sure)  |  might be / might have (~50% uncertain)  |  can't be / can't have (100% impossible)
