SET 6 · REVERSAL MARKERS
"Quite the opposite" / "If anything" / "Far from it" / "The reverse is true"
OET Listening Signal — Reversal / Contrast
KWYT the OP-uh-zit | if AN-ee-thing | FAR from it | the ri-VURS is true
🎵 All four peak on their key content word — "OP" (opposite), "AN" (anything), "FAR", "VURS" (reverse). Each peaks then falls into a short tail. In listening, the pitch drops slightly after the peak — a confident, matter-of-fact delivery that flatly contradicts what came before.
🚨 OET LISTENING KEY RULE:
All four are REVERSAL SIGNALS — discard the information before them and focus entirely on what follows. The content after these phrases is always the clinically accurate / answer-relevant information.
Four phrases with the same function but different emphasis levels. "Quite the opposite" = complete reversal, moderate emphasis. "If anything" = the reality is slightly or significantly worse than implied, often subtle. "Far from it" = strong emphatic contradiction — the reality is very different. "The reverse is true" = the most formal and definitive — used in writing and presentations. In OET listening, "if anything" is particularly dangerous because it sounds mild but signals a significant change in direction.
OET context — "quite the opposite"
"The new dose was meant to reduce the fatigue — quite the opposite happened and she could barely stay awake."
OET context — "if anything"
"She was not improving. If anything, her breathing had become more laboured over the past 24 hours."
📌 Reversal signal family — all mean "the opposite is true":
"quite the opposite"  |  "if anything"  |  "far from it"  |  "the reverse is true"  |  "on the contrary"
SET 6 · OVEREXERTION IDIOMS
"I overdid it" / "It caught up with me" / "I'm feeling it now"
Consequence of Overexertion · Patient Language
I OH-vuh-did it | it KAWT up with me | I'm FEEL-ing it NOW
🎵 "Overdid" — peak on "OH" (long /əʊ/). "Caught up" — peak on "KAWT" (British RP /ɔː/, "aw" sound). "Feeling it now" — two peaks on "FEEL" and "NOW", with a slightly rueful or resigned tone.
💡 Clinical equivalent for documentation:
All three = "patient exceeded physical capacity, resulting in increased pain/fatigue" — document as "reports symptom exacerbation following physical overexertion"
Three natural patient phrases describing the same event — doing too much and suffering the consequences. "I overdid it" = active self-acknowledgement of having exceeded limits. "It caught up with me" = implies a delay between the overexertion and the consequence — they felt fine at the time but later paid the price. "I'm feeling it now" = the symptoms have arrived and are currently present — said with a slightly wry or resigned tone. All three tell the clinician the patient has had a recent exacerbation of symptoms linked to activity.
"I overdid it"
"I went back to the gym too soon and really overdid it — I was in bed for two days afterwards."
"It caught up with me"
"I felt fine during the shift but by evening it had caught up with me — complete exhaustion."
📌 Full overexertion consequence family — all mean "I did too much and now I'm suffering for it":
"I overdid it"  |  "it caught up with me"  |  "I'm feeling it now"  |  "paid for it"  |  "I pushed too hard"
SET 6 · NEW
"All of a sudden" — NOT "all of THE sudden"
Sudden Onset Timing Expression · Common Error
awl uv uh SUD-un
/ɔːl əv ə ˈsʌdən/
🎵 Pitch contour: low-low-low-HIGH-low — peak on "SUD" (/ʌ/ vowel, same as "cup"). The whole phrase builds quietly with three unstressed syllables before the peak. In fast speech: "awl-uvuh-SUD-un" — blends into four smooth syllables.
⚠️ Common error — correct form:
"All of a sudden" ✅  |  "All of the sudden" ❌ — "the sudden" is not standard English
"All of a sudden" is the standard fixed phrase meaning suddenly and unexpectedly — equivalent to "suddenly" but more dramatic and conversational in tone. "All of the sudden" is a very common error, especially among non-native speakers and even some American English speakers, but it is not considered standard. In OET listening, this phrase signals a sudden-onset event — whenever you hear it, what follows is a key clinical symptom or turning point in the narrative.
OET Listening — sudden symptom onset
"All of a sudden she lost the ability to speak and her right arm went completely limp."
General context
"He had been feeling fine all morning — then all of a sudden a severe headache came on without any warning."
📌 Sudden onset signal words in OET listening — all cue you to the key event:
"all of a sudden"  |  "out of nowhere"  |  "without warning"  |  "suddenly"  |  "all at once"
SET 6 · NEW
"Sorted itself out"
Spontaneous Resolution · Patient Language
SOR-tid it-self OWT
British (RP): /ˈsɔːtɪd ɪtˈself aʊt/
🎵 Pitch contour: HIGH-low-low-HIGH — two peaks. "SOR" peaks first (British RP /ɔː/, "aw" sound), then "OWT" peaks at the end (diphthong /aʊ/). The final peak on "out" gives the phrase a sense of completion and relief.
Used when a symptom or problem resolved on its own without any medical intervention — the clinical equivalent of "spontaneous resolution". A very natural patient phrase, often said with slight surprise or relief. Clinically important to hear: it tells you the patient self-managed, no treatment was given, and the condition may be self-limiting. Also useful as a contrast: "I thought it would sort itself out, but it didn't" = the patient delayed seeking care.
Spontaneous resolution
"I was worried at first but the rash sorted itself out within a few days without any treatment."
Delayed help-seeking pattern
"I kept thinking it would sort itself out, but after three weeks I realised I needed to come in."
📌 Clinical equivalent + related patient phrases:
"sorted itself out" = spontaneous resolution  |  "cleared up on its own"  |  "went away by itself"  |  "resolved without treatment"
SET 6 · NEW
"Your child is very sick. You need to know that. This is a Hail Mary pass."
Breaking Bad News · Direct Severity Disclosure
your CHYLD is very SIK | you NEED to know that | this is a HAYL Mary pass
🎵 Three separate sentences — each with its own falling, declarative pitch. "CHYLD" and "SIK" both peak with finality. "NEED" peaks with quiet urgency. "HAYL" peaks sharply — the drama of the phrase is compressed into that one syllable. Each sentence is short, clear, and delivers its meaning without hedging.
💡 Why three short sentences?
Short declarative sentences in bad-news delivery allow the listener to absorb each piece of information before the next arrives. Long complex sentences in a crisis moment are hard to process — brevity is kindness.
"Your child is very sick" — direct severity statement, no minimising. Essential in OET Speaking: don't soften to the point of obscuring the message. "You need to know that" — underlines that this is not a casual observation but something the parent must genuinely understand and act on. "This is a Hail Mary pass" — from American football: a long desperate throw at the end of a game with little chance of success but no other option. Used to describe a last-resort treatment that may work but cannot be promised. Together these three sentences form a complete, compassionate but unflinching bad-news structure.
Full context — House MD style
"Your child is very sick. You need to know that. We have one more option left — but I want to be honest with you: this is a Hail Mary pass."
OET Speaking application
"Your child is very sick and needs hospital admission today. You need to know that — this is not something we can manage at home."
📌 Short declarative bad-news structure — OET Speaking principle:
State severity clearly → confirm the parent heard it → explain the next step plainly → pause and invite questions
SET 6 · NEW
"She thought she had mono but she's actually pregnant"
Diagnostic Error Pattern · Symptom Overlap
she THAWT she had MOH-noh | but she's AK-shoo-uh-lee PREG-nunt
🎵 Two contrasting clauses: "THAWT" and "MOH" peak in the first half — confident assertion of the patient's self-diagnosis. Then "AK" (actually) peaks with emphasis, signalling the correction. "PREG" peaks finally — the punchline/reveal. The rhythm of this sentence mirrors the clinical reveal pattern.
💡 Why mono and pregnancy are confused:
Both cause: fatigue · sore throat · swollen glands · nausea · general malaise — same symptom cluster, different cause
"Mono" = mononucleosis (infectious mononucleosis, also called glandular fever) — caused by the Epstein-Barr virus. "MOH-noh" — stress on 1st syllable. A classic OET and clinical scenario demonstrating that patient self-diagnosis is unreliable and that overlapping symptom profiles can lead to missed diagnoses. The key teaching point: always verify. Never accept a patient's self-reported diagnosis without independent confirmation. A pregnancy test is simple, fast, and essential before prescribing several medications.
Diagnostic pattern — symptom overlap
"She thought she had mono — the fatigue and sore throat fitted — but she was actually pregnant. The pregnancy test came back positive."
Clinical lesson application
The case was a reminder to always run a pregnancy test before prescribing: she thought she had mono, which would have led to a very different management plan.
📌 Clinical principle — always verify self-diagnosis:
Patient says "I have X" → clinician confirms or rules out X independently before acting on it

SET 5 · NEW
"Away with the fairies"
Mental State · British Colloquial
a-WAY with the FAIR-eez
🎵 Pitch: peak on "WAY", second peak on "FAIR". "With the" sits very low between them — almost swallowed. The whole phrase has a light, slightly whimsical rhythm that matches its meaning (mentally drifting/elsewhere).
🔊 Fast speech sounds like:
"uh-WAY-with-thuh-FAIR-eez" — "with the" reduces to "with-thuh"
A distinctly British idiom for being mentally absent, confused, or disconnected — often used about the effects of sedating medication, fever, or severe fatigue. Lighter and more affectionate in tone than "confused" or "disoriented" — often used by family members about a loved one rather than by clinicians in formal notes. Clinical equivalent: "confused", "disconnected from reality", "not fully present".
OET context
"The sedative made him feel completely away with the fairies — he could not hold a normal conversation for the first few hours."
Family context
"My grandmother's a bit away with the fairies today — the new pain medication seems to be affecting her."
📌 Clinical equivalents (formal documentation):
confused / disconnected / not fully oriented / mentally clouded
SET 5 · NEW
"I feel under the weather" / "I feel run down"
General Unwellness · Paired Expressions
under the WETH-uh | feel RUN down
🎵 "Under the weather" — peak on "WETH" (voiced "th" /ð/, like "this"/"the"), the rest of the phrase falls gently — sounds mild and understated. "Run down" — peak on "RUN", "down" falls — short and slightly heavier in tone than "under the weather", implying a more depleted, ongoing state.
💡 Key distinction:
"under the weather" = short-term, mild dip in condition  |  "run down" = long-term, accumulated fatigue/depletion
Both are very common patient self-reports of non-specific unwellness, but they differ in duration and severity. "Under the weather" suggests a mild, often short-term dip — like the early stage of a cold. "Run down" implies a more prolonged depletion — chronic fatigue, poor sleep, inadequate nutrition built up over time. Listening for which one a patient uses can hint at how long the problem has been going on.
Short-term, mild
She phoned in saying she had felt under the weather all week and was not sure if she should take her usual medication.
Long-term, depleted
She had been feeling run down for weeks — fatigue, poor appetite, and difficulty concentrating.
SET 5 · NEW
"Sweating buckets" / "Throwing up"
Symptom Emphasis · Colloquial
SWET-ing BUK-its | THROH-ing UP
🎵 "Sweating buckets" — two peaks, "SWET" and "BUK", both said with extra force to emphasise the sheer volume. "Throwing up" — peak on "THROH" (diphthong /əʊ/), "UP" gets a sharp final emphasis (phrasal verb particle).
🔊 Fast speech sounds like:
"SWET-ing-BUK-its" | "THROH-ing-UP" — both phrases keep their stress even when spoken quickly
Vivid intensifying expressions — "buckets" emphasises an extreme volume of sweat (informal hyperbole, like "sweating a lot" but stronger). "Throwing up" is the standard informal term for vomiting, used constantly by patients instead of the clinical word "vomiting". Both signal that the patient considers the symptom severe enough to be worth emphasising strongly, not just mentioning in passing.
OET context — fever/infection
He described sweating buckets throughout the night — drenching his sheets repeatedly.
OET context — vomiting
He reported throwing up all day yesterday — oral medication was not viable.
📌 Clinical equivalents:
"sweating buckets" → profuse sweating / diaphoresis  |  "throwing up" → vomiting
SET 5 · NEW
"I can't keep anything down"
Digestive Symptom · Colloquial
I can't KEEP anything DOWN
🎵 Pitch: peak on "KEEP" (the key verb), second peak on "DOWN" (the phrasal particle that completes the meaning). Without "down", "keep anything" doesn't carry the same meaning — both peaks are essential to understanding.
🔊 Fast speech sounds like:
"I-KANT-KEEP-EN-ee-thing-DOWN"
"Keep something down" (phrasal verb) = to retain food or liquid in the stomach without vomiting. This phrase signals an inability to tolerate ANY oral intake — clinically significant for dehydration risk and for whether oral medication is even a viable route. When a patient says this, it's a strong cue that IV fluids or anti-emetics may be needed before anything else can be addressed.
OET context
She called the pharmacy to report she could not keep anything down — every dose was immediately vomited back up.
Follow-up question
"If you genuinely can't keep anything down, including water, you may need IV fluids rather than oral medication."
📌 Clinical equivalent:
unable to tolerate oral intake / persistent vomiting preventing oral intake
SET 5 · NEW
"It didn't get any better — in fact quite the opposite"
Treatment Failure Emphasis
didn't get any BET-ter | KWYT the OP-uh-zit
🎵 First clause peaks on "BET-ter" with a slightly rising, hopeful-sounding pitch that gets cut off. Second clause — "quite the opposite" — peaks hard on both "KWYT" and "OP", with a falling, definitive tone that flatly contradicts the first half. This contrast in pitch pattern mirrors the contrast in meaning.
🚨 OET listening signal:
"In fact quite the opposite" REVERSES the expectation set up by the first half of the sentence — the second half is always the true/answer-relevant information.
A two-part contrast structure: state an expected improvement, then sharply reverse it. "Quite the opposite" is a strong reversal marker — similar function to "that said" or "if anything" from earlier sets, but more emphatic and definitive. In OET listening, always trust the SECOND half of this kind of sentence as the accurate clinical picture.
OET context
He reported that the pain had not got any better — in fact quite the opposite — it had doubled in intensity overnight.
Treatment review context
"I was hoping the new dose would help, but it didn't get any better — in fact quite the opposite — the swelling got worse."
📌 Same reversal-marker family:
"quite the opposite"  |  "if anything"  |  "far from it"  |  "the reverse is true"
SET 5 · NEW
"I was in too much pain to drive"
Functional Limitation Expression
too much PAYN to DRYVE
🎵 Pitch: peak on "PAYN", second peak on "DRYVE" — "to" sits very low between them, almost disappearing. The structure "too [adjective] to [verb]" is a common pattern where both content words at the ends of the phrase get full stress.
This "too X to Y" structure is extremely common in patient self-reports for describing functional limitations caused by symptoms — it directly links the symptom to a real-world consequence, which is often what clinicians (and OET examiners) are listening for. The structure can be applied to almost any activity: "too breathless to climb stairs", "too dizzy to stand", "too nauseous to eat".
OET context
He had been unable to attend his follow-up appointment — he was in too much pain to drive.
Functional impact context
"I missed work again this week — I was in too much pain to even get out of bed some mornings."
📌 Same "too X to Y" functional limitation pattern:
"too breathless to climb stairs"  |  "too dizzy to stand"  |  "too tired to cook"  |  "too nauseous to eat"
SET 5 · NEW
"I can't say they made much difference"
Treatment Ineffectiveness · Understatement
can't SAY they made much DIF-runts
🎵 Pitch: peak on "SAY", second peak on "DIF". The phrase has a soft, hedging quality — the negation isn't stated bluntly ("they didn't work") but is wrapped in a more polite, indirect structure. This softer pitch pattern matches the softer, more polite phrasing.
A classic British understatement — instead of saying directly "they didn't work" or "they were useless", the patient hedges with "I can't say they made much difference." This is functionally a negative statement about treatment effectiveness, just delivered more politely. In OET listening, recognise this as equivalent to "ineffective" or "no significant benefit" when documenting treatment response.
OET context
She had tried three different over-the-counter pain relievers but could not say they had made much difference.
Follow-up assessment
"I've been using the cream as directed, but I can't say it's made much difference to the itching."
📌 Clinical equivalent:
"made minimal/no difference" → "treatment was ineffective" / "no significant clinical benefit observed"
SET 5 · NEW
"I have a few niggles" / "Just a bit of a niggle"
Minor Residual Symptom Expression
a few NIG-ulz | a bit of a NIG-ul
🎵 Both peak on "NIG" — short and light, matching the minor, non-serious nature of what's being described. The diminutive structure "a bit of a..." softens it even further than "a few niggles" — say it with a slightly dismissive, casual tone.
💡 Singular vs plural:
"a few niggles" = several minor issues, possibly in different spots  |  "a bit of a niggle" = one single minor, ongoing discomfort
"Niggle" is a quintessentially British understatement word for a minor, persistent, but not seriously limiting discomfort — typically used for post-surgical or post-injury residual symptoms that are improving but not fully resolved. It signals the patient is largely recovered but not 100%. Important to follow up on, since "niggles" can sometimes mask a developing problem that the patient is minimising.
OET context
He said he had a few niggles in his knee when descending stairs but otherwise managed well.
OET context — singular
She said it was just a bit of a niggle — not enough to stop her working but enough to notice every day.
SET 5 · NEW
"Paid for it"
Consequence / Causation Colloquial
PAYD for it
🎵 Pitch: hard peak on "PAYD" (diphthong /eɪ/), then a quick fall on "for it" — almost wry or rueful in tone. Often said with a slight laugh or sigh, acknowledging the consequences of overdoing it.
An idiom meaning "experienced negative consequences as a direct result of an action" — most often used when a patient has pushed themselves too hard physically and suffered increased pain or fatigue as a result. Distinct from literal financial payment — this is entirely metaphorical. Common after physiotherapy, exercise, or any activity that exceeded the patient's current capacity.
OET context
He had pushed through his physiotherapy session and really paid for it — he was unable to walk the next day.
Activity recovery context
"I went back to the gym a bit too soon and definitely paid for it — I could barely move for two days."

"What's brought you here today?"
Clinical Expression
what's BROUGHT you HERE toDAY
🎵 Pitch: rises on "BROUGHT", dips slightly, rises again on "HERE", falls on "today". The stress lands on content words (brought, here, today), not function words (what's, you).
🔊 Fast speech sounds like:
"wots BRAWT-chuh-HERE-tuh-DAY?" — "you" reduces to "yuh"; "to" almost disappears
Standard clinical opening used by doctors to invite the patient's story. Slightly warmer than "What is your complaint?" — the verb "brought" implies the patient was compelled to come by something serious. Doctors also say: "What can I do for you today?" / "What seems to be the problem?"
OET context
"Mr Mortlake, I have your notes here, but perhaps you can tell me in your own words — what's brought you here today?"
Opening a new consultation
"Good morning — before we start, what's brought you here today?"

"What seems to be the problem?"  |  "How can I help you today?"  |  "Tell me in your own words..."

that SAID | even SO | having said THAT | then aGAIN | MIND you
