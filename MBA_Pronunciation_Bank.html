ASSOCIATE DEGREE
uh-SOH-see-ut di-GREE 6 syllables total
British RP: /əˈsəʊsiət dɪˈɡriː/
🎵 Pitch: low-SOH-low-low | low-GREE — two peaks. "Associate" peaks on "SOH"; "degree" peaks on "GREE" (final, longest peak — "GREE" has a long /iː/ that stretches). "Uh-" is a very short schwa onset. "-see-ut" runs quickly after the peak.
🔊 Fast speech sounds like:
"uh-SOH-see-ut-duh-GREE" — "di-" reduces to "duh"
⚠️ "Associate" = uh-SOH-see-ut (4 syllables, stress on 2nd "SOH"). As a verb, it's uh-SOH-see-ayt (slightly different ending) — as a noun/adjective, the "-ate" reduces to "-ut". "Degree" = di-GREE (stress on 2nd, long /iː/ at end). An associate degree = typically a 2-year qualification from a community college or polytechnic, below a bachelor's degree. In NZ/Australia context: roughly equivalent to a diploma or foundation degree. Key distinction for academic accreditation purposes: associate degree < bachelor's degree < postgraduate diploma < master's degree.
📋 Academic context: Often relevant when discussing credit recognition, pathway programmes, or prior learning assessment. "Her associate degree was only partially recognized by the university, requiring her to complete additional credits."
Example 1
She completed an associate degree in health sciences before enrolling in the bachelor of pharmacy programme.
Example 2
"An associate degree is a two-year qualification — it sits below a bachelor's degree but can often be used to gain credit towards one."
📌 Academic qualification ladder (NZ/UK context):
certificate → diploma → associate degree → bachelor's degree → postgraduate diploma → master's degree → doctorate (PhD)

STATS (non-clinical)
STATS 1 syllable
/stæts/
🎵 Pitch: single syllable — one sharp peak. STATS = short /æ/ vowel as in "cat", "back", "bad". Ends with a clear /ts/ cluster — don't drop the "t": "STATS" not "STAZ". In a phrase: "my exam STATS improved" — "STATS" gets the main sentence stress as the key noun.
🔊 Note — two different contexts:
OET (clinical) → "vital stats" = vital signs → in OET Pronunciation Bank VOCAB file
MBA/general → "exam stats", "sales stats" = statistics/numbers → this file
⚠️ "Stats" = short /æ/ (NOT "storts" or "stahts"). Final consonant cluster /ts/ — both sounds must be clear. "Stats" is an informal plural noun — always used in plural form ("the stats show..." NOT "the stat shows..."). Context determines meaning: clinical setting = vital signs; business/academic setting = data/numbers/figures.
Example 1 — academic
My exam stats improved significantly after I changed my revision strategy and started using active recall.
Example 2 — business
The sales stats for Q3 exceeded projections by 14% — the new pricing strategy appears to be working.
📌 "Stats" in different professional contexts:
clinical → "vital stats" (OET file)  |  business → "sales stats / revenue stats"  |  academic → "exam stats / grade stats"  |  sports → "player stats"

ACCEPTABLE FOR DEGREE RECOGNITION
ak-SEP-tuh-bl | fuh | di-GREE | rek-ug-NISH-un
acceptable /əkˈseptəbl/ | recognition /ˌrekəɡˈnɪʃən/
🎵 Three word-peaks: SEP (in acceptable) / GREE (in degree) / NISH (in recognition). "For" reduces to "fuh". "Recognition" = rek-ug-NISH-un (stress on 3rd syllable "NISH") — 4 syllables.
⚠️ "Acceptable" = ak-SEP-tuh-bl (stress on 2nd "SEP"; "-able" = "-uh-bl"). "Recognition" = rek-ug-NISH-un — "rec-" = /rek/, "-og-" = /əɡ/ (schwa), "-ni-" = /nɪ/, "-tion" = /ʃən/. Academic admin phrase: used in the context of overseas qualification assessment, RPL (Recognition of Prior Learning), or credit transfer. "Acceptable for degree recognition" = the qualification meets the standard required to be counted toward a degree programme.
Example 1
The overseas nursing qualification was deemed acceptable for degree recognition, allowing her to receive advanced standing in the programme.
Example 2
"Is my diploma from Korea acceptable for degree recognition here, or will I need to complete bridging courses?"
📌 Related academic accreditation terms:
"recognition of prior learning (RPL)"  |  "credit transfer"  |  "advanced standing"  |  "equivalent qualification"  |  "bridging course"
SET 1 · NEW
PRACTICUM
PRAK-ti-kum 3 syllables
/ˈpræktɪkəm/
🎵 Pitch: HIGH-low-low — peak on "PRAK", then falls through "-ti-kum". "PRAK" = /præk/ — short /æ/ vowel. "-ti-" = quick /tɪ/. "-kum" = schwa + m. Three syllables, only the first matters.
⚠️ Stress on 1st syllable "PRAK". Short /æ/ vowel ("cat" vowel). "-ticum" = "-ti-kum" (the "a" in "-icum" is a schwa). "Practicum" = a supervised practical component of an academic programme — more formal/academic than "placement" or "internship". Common in: teaching, pharmacy, nursing, social work, counselling programmes. In NZ pharmacy context: the practicum = supervised hours in a pharmacy setting required for registration. Distinguished from "internship" (usually paid, business-focused) and "placement" (more general British English term).
Example 1
Students are required to complete a practicum of 400 hours in an approved pharmacy before they can sit the registration examination.
Example 2
"Will my hospital work experience in Korea count towards the practicum requirement, or do I need to complete additional hours here?"
📌 Supervised practical training — terminology comparison:
"practicum" = academic/formal  |  "placement" = British general term  |  "internship" = often paid, business  |  "clinical attachment" = medical/health specific
SET 1 · NEW
ELIGIBLE
EL-i-ji-bl 4 syllables
/ˈelɪdʒɪbl/
🎵 Pitch: HIGH-low-low-low — peak on "EL", then falls through three quiet syllables. "-i-" = /ɪ/ (short). "-ji-" = /dʒɪ/ (soft "j" sound — same "g" as in "digoxin"). "-bl" = "-bul" (schwa). The word ends very lightly — the final "-ble" is almost swallowed.
⚠️ Stress on 1st syllable "EL". The "-gible" = "-ji-bul" — the "g" is soft /dʒ/ (like "jelly", "gym"). "-ble" reduces to "-bul" (schwa). Common error: "el-i-GI-bul" (stressing wrong syllable) or "el-i-JEE-bul" (making "-gi-" too long). "Eligible" = meeting the requirements to qualify for something. Opposite: "ineligible" = in-EL-i-ji-bul. Academic use: "eligible for advanced standing", "eligible to apply", "eligible for credit recognition".
Example 1
Applicants who completed their degree within the past seven years are eligible to apply for the bridging programme.
Example 2
"Am I eligible to have my Korean pharmacy degree recognised here, or do I need to complete additional qualifications first?"
📌 Same "-gible" soft /dʒ/ pattern:
eligible → EL-i-ji-bul  |  legible → LEJ-i-bul  |  negligible → NEG-li-ji-bul

CLARIFICATION PHRASES: "Does that sound right?" / "Would that be enough to obtain...?" / "Does she have to follow her original major?"
SOUND right | E-nuf | FOL-oh her OR-i-ji-nul MAY-juh
Key: sound /saʊnd/ | enough /ɪˈnʌf/ | major /ˈmeɪdʒə/
🎵 "Does that sound right?" — rises on SOUND, then "right" gets a questioning rise (upward intonation at end = yes/no question). "Would that be enough?" — peaks on E-nuf with rising question intonation at end. "Major" = MAY-juh — British RP: "-jor" = "-juh" (non-rhotic, no "r" sound).
These three phrases share a common function: checking understanding / seeking confirmation. Essential for academic admin conversations (with registrars, advisors, accreditation bodies). "Does that sound right?" = checking your own understanding with the other person. "Would that be enough?" = asking whether a minimum threshold is met. "Does she have to follow her original major?" = asking about a rule/requirement for a third party. British RP: "major" = MAY-juh (the "-or" is non-rhotic — sounds like "-uh").
Checking understanding
"So I need to complete six additional credits before I can apply for graduation — does that sound right?"
Asking about sufficiency
"If I finish these two remaining units, would that be enough to obtain the Level 2 certificate?"
Asking about rules for a third party
"Does she have to follow her original major when earning additional credits, or can she choose from other departments?"
📌 Same "confirmation check" pattern — useful in any professional context:
"Does that sound right?"  |  "Is that correct?"  |  "Have I understood that correctly?"  |  "So what you're saying is...?"
SET 1 · NEW
"WHEN IT COMES TO" — usage note
when it COMES to
/wen ɪt ˈkʌmz tə/
🎵 Pitch: "when it" is very light, peak on COMES, "to" drops to a schwa. The whole phrase is a low-stress opener — the real stress comes on whatever follows it. "When it comes to CREDIT recognition..." — "CREDIT" gets the main sentence stress.
🔊 Usage note from class:
"When it comes to" is not wrong, but can sound wordy and indirect in formal writing. More direct alternatives often preferred in MBA/academic contexts.
"When it comes to [X]" = regarding X / in terms of X / as for X. Grammatically correct and natural in spoken English. However, in formal academic or business writing, it can sound conversational and indirect. Preferred alternatives: "Regarding..." / "In terms of..." / "With respect to..." / "As for..." — all more concise. In SPEAKING (presentations, meetings): "when it comes to" is fine and sounds natural. In WRITING (reports, essays, emails): replace with a more direct construction.
Spoken (acceptable)
"When it comes to credit recognition, the process can take several weeks."
Written (more direct)
"Regarding credit recognition, applicants should allow several weeks for processing." [preferred in formal writing]
📌 Direct alternatives (formal writing):
"Regarding..."  |  "In terms of..."  |  "With respect to..."  |  "As for..."  |  "Concerning..."  |  "On the subject of..."

ACADEMIC ACTION VERBS: "register credits" / "apply for credit recognition" / "submit for certification"
REJ-is-tuh KRED-its | uh-PLY for KRED-it rek-ug-NISH-un | sub-MIT for sur-ti-fi-KAY-shun
register /ˈredʒɪstə/ | recognition /ˌrekəɡˈnɪʃən/ | certification /ˌsɜːtɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
🎵 Three distinct verb phrases — each with its own peak pattern. "Register" = REJ-is-tuh (peak on 1st; British RP "-er" = "-tuh", no r sound). "Apply" = uh-PLY (peak on 2nd). "Recognition" = rek-ug-NISH-un (peak on 3rd). "Submit" = sub-MIT (peak on 2nd). "Certification" = sur-ti-fi-KAY-shun (peak on 4th).
Three distinct actions in the academic credit process — often confused or used interchangeably but have different meanings. "Register credits" = enrol in / officially record credits in the system. "Apply for credit recognition" = formally request that previous study be counted (RPL process). "Submit for certification" = send completed paperwork/evidence to receive an official certificate. British RP: "register" = REJ-is-tuh (soft "g" = /dʒ/; "-er" = schwa, no r). "Certification" = sur-ti-fi-KAY-shun (6 syllables; "-cer-" = "sur-" non-rhotic).
Register credits
You can register credits through the student portal — make sure to do this before the enrolment deadline.
Apply for credit recognition
To apply for credit recognition, you will need to submit your official transcripts and a course outline from your previous institution.
Submit for certification
Once all requirements are met, you can submit for certification — processing typically takes four to six weeks.
📌 Academic credit process — sequential actions:
apply for credit recognition → receive assessment → register approved credits → complete remaining units → submit for certification

"I tried to register for the 8 subjects I haven't completed yet" / "Which option would be better?" / "Are lesser-known institutes a good option?"
REJ-is-tuh for | WHICH OP-shun | LES-uh-nohn IN-sti-tyoots
lesser-known /ˈlesənəʊn/ | institutes /ˈɪnstɪtjuːts/
🎵 "Which option would be better?" — peaks on WHICH and BET-tuh; rising intonation at end (yes/no question). "Lesser-known" = LES-uh-nohn — compound adjective, stress on first element. "Institutes" = IN-sti-tyoots — British RP: "-tutes" = "-tyoots" (with /j/ glide).
"Register for subjects" = enrol in specific courses (note: "register FOR subjects" not "register subjects"). "Lesser-known" = not widely recognised / not well established — compound adjective, hyphenated before a noun. "Institutes" = IN-sti-tyoots (British RP /j/ glide: "tyoots" not "toots"). Key concern with lesser-known institutes for credit recognition: accreditation bodies may not accept credits from unrecognised providers — always check whether the institute is NZQA-approved or equivalent before enrolling.
Enrolling in remaining subjects
"I tried to register for the eight subjects I haven't completed yet, but the system wouldn't let me — apparently I need advisor approval first."
Comparing options
"Which option would be better — completing the credits through the university directly, or using a third-party provider?"
Asking about provider quality
"Are lesser-known institutes a good option, or is there a risk that the credits won't be recognised by the accreditation body?"
📌 Key question when choosing a provider:
Is it NZQA-approved?  |  Will credits be recognised?  |  Is it accredited by the relevant professional body?  |  Does it appear on the approved provider list?

LIFELONG EDUCATION INSTITUTE
LYFE-long | ed-yoo-KAY-shun | IN-sti-tyoot 9 syllables total
lifelong /ˈlaɪflɒŋ/ | education /ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/ | institute /ˈɪnstɪtjuːt/
🎵 Three word-peaks: LYFE-long / ed-yoo-KAY-shun / IN-sti-tyoot. "Lifelong" = compound: stress on first element LYFE. "Education" = 5 syllables: ed-yoo-KAY-shun — British RP has /j/ glide: "ed-YOO" not "ed-OO". "Institute" = IN-sti-tyoot (British RP: "-tute" = "-tyoot" with /j/ glide).
"Lifelong education institute" refers to an institution offering continuing education for adult learners, often outside traditional university settings. This concept is common in South Korea (where such institutes are typically attached to universities) and is increasingly found in NZ/Australia. Key question for credit recognition: is the institute accredited? Credits from non-accredited providers may not be transferable. British RP: "education" = ed-yoo-KAY-shun (5 syllables, not 4 — the "u" after "ed" creates a separate syllable with /j/ glide).
Example 1
The lifelong education institute attached to the university offers evening courses that can count towards a formal qualification.
Example 2
"I completed some units through a lifelong education institute in Korea — would those credits be recognised here for the purpose of degree completion?"
📌 British RP /j/ glide in "-tion" words with "u":
education → ed-YOO-KAY-shun  |  institution → in-sti-TYOO-shun  |  solution → suh-LYOO-shun
SET 2 · NEW
"DURING THIS PERIOD" — period vs term
dur-ing this PEER-ee-ud
period /ˈpɪəriəd/ | term /tɜːm/
🎵 "During this period" — "during this" is low and quick; peak on PEER-ee-ud. "Period" = PEER-ee-ud (3 syllables, stress on 1st). The /ɪə/ diphthong in "PEER" = starts at "ih" and glides to "uh" — same as "here", "near", "clear". British RP: "-riod" = "-ree-ud" (3 syllables total).
🔊 Period vs Term — key distinction:
period = any duration of time (general)  |  term = a specific academic semester/trimester (structured, calendar-fixed)
"Period" = a duration of time — general, flexible, not tied to an academic calendar. "During this period" = during this time / over this duration. "Term" = a fixed academic unit (semester/trimester) — structured, calendar-specific. Common mistake: using "term" when you mean a general time span ("during the practicum term" → should be "during the practicum period"). In NZ: universities typically use "semester" (not "term" — which is more common in UK secondary schools). Use "period" when referring to a duration of activity; use "semester" when referring to an academic calendar unit.
Correct — general duration
"During this period, you will be required to complete a minimum of 400 supervised clinical hours." ✅
Incorrect — wrongly using "term"
"During this term, you will be required to complete the practicum hours." ❌ (unless referring to a specific semester)
Correct — using "semester" for academic calendar
"The practicum must be completed within the first semester of enrolment." ✅
📌 Time reference vocabulary — choose precisely:
"period" = any duration  |  "semester" = NZ/US academic unit  |  "term" = UK school unit  |  "trimester" = 3-part academic year  |  "academic year" = full year

MAJOR
MAY-juh 2 syllables
British (RP): /ˈmeɪdʒə/
🎵 Pitch contour: HIGH-low — peak on "MAY" (diphthong /eɪ/). British RP non-rhotic: "-jor" = "-juh" (no r sound, schwa ending). The "j" = soft /dʒ/ as in "gem".

A flexible high-frequency word used across academic writing, business reports, and clinical documentation. "Major" before a noun adds weight without overstating: "a major concern" is stronger than "a significant concern" but less dramatic than "a critical concern".
MBA / academic writing
This represents a major shift in how healthcare is delivered at the community level.
Clinical / OET context
The patient's major concern was the risk of dependency — she had seen a family member struggle with opioids.
📌 Strength scale for emphasis — choose precisely:
notable (mild) → significant → major → substantial → critical / pivotal (strongest)

MAJORITY
muh-JOR-i-tee 4 syllables
British (RP): /məˈdʒɒrɪti/
🎵 Pitch contour: low-HIGH-low-low — peak on "JOR" (British RP /ɒ/, "hot" vowel, non-rhotic — no r sound). Falls through "-i-tee".
⚠️ Stress on 2nd syllable "JOR". British RP non-rhotic: "JOR" = /dʒɒ/ — no r sound, short /ɒ/ vowel (same as "hot", "lot"). More than half of a total — used constantly in research writing, data presentation, and policy analysis. "The majority of" = most of. Pair with a singular or plural noun depending on context — in British English "majority" takes a plural verb when referring to people: "The majority of patients were..."
Research / academic writing
The majority of patients in the study reported improved outcomes after six weeks of treatment.
Policy / MBA context
The majority of respondents indicated that wait times were their primary concern with the current healthcare system.
📌 Proportion vocabulary — from least to most:
a minority (<50%) → just over half → the majority (>50%) → the vast majority → almost all / nearly all

CAUSATION VERBS: "determine" / "influence" / "affect" / "driven by" / "attributed to"
di-TUR-min | IN-floo-uns | uh-FEKT | DRIV-un by | uh-TRIB-yoo-tid to
determine /dɪˈtɜːmɪn/ | influence /ˈɪnfluəns/ | affect /əˈfekt/
🎵 "Determine" peaks on "TUR" (British RP non-rhotic — no r sound). "Influence" peaks on "IN" (1st syllable noun/verb same). "Affect" peaks on "FEKT" (2nd syllable — verb form; note "EFF-ekt" as a noun has different stress). "Driven by" — peaks on "DRIV". "Attributed to" — peaks on "TRIB".
A causation-strength ladder, ranked from weakest to strongest link: affect (some impact, doesn't fully change outcome) → influence (shapes direction/degree) → attributed to (careful analytical claim, one factor among several) → driven by (emphasises primary force) → determine (causes/decides the outcome directly). Choosing the right verb signals precision in MBA writing — using "determine" when you mean "affect" overstates your claim.
Weak link
Weather affects sales — but it's only one of many factors.
Moderate link

MBA · NEW
CAUSATIVE STRUCTURE: CAUSE / LEAD TO / RESULT IN
⚠️ Grammar pattern essential for OET writing and MBA reports — THINGS cause nouns / lead to nouns / result in nouns — never cause to + verb. Things (smoking fever stress) → cause + noun. People → cause + person + to + verb.
Example
Prolonged stress leads to burnout which results in increased absenteeism and reduced productivity.
MBA · NEW
STATIVE VERBS: CONSIST OF / CONTAIN / INCLUDE / INVOLVE
⚠️ Stative verbs describe states not actions — never use is before them — simply add -s for third-person singular: consists of / contains / includes / involves. Common error: our body is consisted of — correct: our body consists of.
Example
The programme consists of three phases: assessment, intervention, and evaluation.
MBA · NEW
TRANSITIVE VERBS THAT NEVER TAKE A PREPOSITION: ACKNOWLEDGE / RECOGNIZE / ACCEPT / CONFIRM / CONSIDER
⚠️ These verbs take a direct object immediately with no preposition — never acknowledge about / recognise about — simply: acknowledge this / recognise the issue. Common error: mixing with know about or learn about which do take about.
Example
The committee acknowledged the need for urgent reform without further deliberation.

MBA · NEW
CARRY OUT
KA-ree owt 3 syllables
British (RP): /ˈkæri aʊt/
⚠️ 'Carry' — stress on KA: KA-ree. Phrasal verb meaning to perform, execute, or complete a task. OET/academic register note: 'carry out' is natural and correct in OET speaking and professional letters. In formal academic writing, prefer 'conduct' or 'undertake' for slightly higher register. All three are acceptable — choose based on context. Examples: 'carry out a biopsy', 'carry out an assessment', 'carry out a medication review'.
Example 1
The pharmacist carried out a full medication review to identify potential interactions before the patient's scheduled surgery.
Example 2
The study was carried out over a 12-month period, involving 200 participants from three community pharmacies.
📌 Register ladder — 'carry out' vs alternatives:
carry out → natural, correct, slightly informal | conduct → formal, academic preferred | undertake → formal, implies commitment | perform → clinical/procedural context | execute → business/project management

DIALLED INTO / B2–C1 LEVEL / PHRASING AND GRAMMAR SLIPS
DYLD IN-too | BEE-TWO to SEE-WUN Phrases — no single stress pattern
British (RP): /daɪəld ˈɪntuː/
⚠️ British spelling: 'dialled' (double-L) ✅ | American: 'dialed'. 'Dialled into' = calibrated to, adjusted for — informal but widely used in language teaching. 'B2–C1' = upper-intermediate to advanced on the CEFR scale. 'Phrasing slips' = slightly unnatural word choices. 'Grammar slips' = minor grammatical errors. OET candidates typically perform at B2–C1 — understanding this helps with self-assessment. Note: OET Grade B ≈ CEFR B2+.
Example 1
The tutor provided a rewrite dialled into B2–C1 level — natural enough to be credible, but with subtle phrasing and grammar slips reflecting the student's current proficiency.
Example 2
Awareness of your own recurring grammar slips — such as missing articles or incorrect prepositions — is the first step toward eliminating them under exam conditions.
📌 CEFR levels — OET equivalence:
A1–A2 = Beginner | B1 = Intermediate | B2 = Upper-intermediate (≈ OET Grade C+) | C1 = Advanced (≈ OET Grade B) | C2 = Proficiency (≈ OET Grade A) | OET target: Grade B = C1 level

VALIDATING (PATIENT CONCERNS / RESEARCH)
VAL-ih-day-ting 4 syllables
British (RP): /ˈvælɪdeɪtɪŋ/
⚠️ Stress on VAL (1st syllable). Do NOT say 'val-IH-day-ting' or 'val-ih-DAY-ting'. Two key uses: (1) OET communication: validating a patient's concerns = acknowledging their feelings as real and understandable BEFORE giving information — a core OET marking criterion. Skipping validation = automatic penalty. (2) Research: validating a tool/questionnaire = confirming it measures what it claims to measure. NEVER move straight to information-giving without validating first.
Example 1
The pharmacist began by validating the patient's concerns — 'I completely understand why this feels frightening' — before moving on to explain the medication.
Example 2
Validating the questionnaire involved testing it with a sample population and comparing the results against an established gold-standard measure.
📌 OET validation — do this BEFORE anything else:
(1) Validate: 'I can understand why you're worried about that' | (2) Normalise: 'Many patients feel the same way' | (3) THEN give information | ❌ Never: 'Don't worry, it's fine' (dismissive) | ❌ Never: jump straight into facts without acknowledging the emotion

INTEGRATIVE
IN-teh-gruh-tiv 4 syllables
British (RP): /ˈɪntɪɡrətɪv/
⚠️ Stress on IN (1st syllable). Do NOT say 'in-TEH-gruh-tiv' or 'in-teh-GRAY-tiv'. Integrative = combining or unifying different elements into a coherent whole. Three key contexts: (1) Research: 'integrative review' = synthesises both quantitative and qualitative studies. (2) MBA: 'integrative thinking' = holding opposing ideas and finding creative resolution (Roger Martin). (3) Clinical: 'integrative medicine' = combining conventional and complementary approaches.
Example 1
The team conducted an integrative review of the literature — synthesising findings from randomised controlled trials and qualitative patient experience studies to inform the new guidelines.
Example 2
Integrative thinking allowed the project team to reconcile the competing demands of cost reduction and quality improvement — finding a solution that achieved both.
📌 Integrative vs related terms:
integrative → combining into a whole (integrative review, integrative thinking) | integrated → already combined (integrated care, integrated project) | integral → essential, fundamental ('an integral part of') | integrity → moral uprightness / structural completeness
