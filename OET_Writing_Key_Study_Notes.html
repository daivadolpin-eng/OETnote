1. Letter Layout

Based on official OET samples — always follow this order.

OrderElementExample

2Address22 Laurel Crescent, Riverdale
3Date21 June 2026
4GreetingDear Mr Whitfield,
5Re: line (include DOB if available)Re: Medication for Mrs Doris Whitfield (DOB: 14/03/1936)

⚠️ Date goes after the address, before Dear — this is the order used in official OET samples.

⚠️ DOB in the Re: line — include if given in the case notes, omit if not.

2. The 6 OET Marking Criteria

CriterionWhat it assessesB-grade benchmark
PurposeIs the purpose of the letter clear?2+ out of 3
ContentIs the necessary clinical information accurate and complete?5+ out of 7
Conciseness & ClarityIs it written concisely, with only essential information?5+ out of 7
Genre & StyleIs the language appropriate for the reader?5+ out of 7
Organisation & LayoutIs the paragraph structure and letter format logical?5+ out of 7
LanguageIs the grammar, vocabulary, and spelling accurate?5+ out of 7

Key principle: if even one of the six criteria is weak, the overall grade is pulled down — balance matters.

3. Word Count

  • Target range: 180–200 words (body only)
  • Date, address, greeting (Dear), and closing (Sincerely) are NOT counted
  • Exceeding the limit directly lowers the Conciseness & Clarity score
  • Main causes of overrunning: abstract introductions, overuse of linking words, repeated phrasing

4. Linking Words — Myth vs Fact

❌ Myth: using Furthermore / Moreover / In addition often raises your score.

✅ Fact: repeating the same linker reads like a memorised template and can actually lower your score.


FunctionLinking words you can use
Additionadditionally, furthermore, in addition, she also
Time/Sequenceon discharge, following this, subsequently, prior to, at the time of
Cause/Resultas a result, consequently, therefore, given that, due to
Contrasthowever, although, despite this, nevertheless
Specificationmore specifically, in particular, notably
Conditionshould [X] occur, if [X] develops, in the event of

Practical tip: don't use the same linker twice in one paragraph. If you can join ideas with a comma + "and" instead, that's often more concise.

5. Introduction — Common Mistake

❌ Weak introduction (two content-free abstract sentences = ~48 wasted words)

"Although you are already well aware of previous medication regimen, the instruction of current medication regimen is necessary to be checked. Following the correct explanation, the usage of newly prescribed medication will be safe and effective."


✅ Strong introduction (purpose + clinical context in one sentence)

"I am writing to explain your new medicines after your recent hospital stay for a COPD flare-up, including a change of inhaler and two new short courses of treatment."


Principle: a longer introduction is fine when filled with real clinical information (diagnosis, drug names, reason for admission) — not when filled with generic statements.

⚠️ "intake" is a noun only — it can never be used as a verb.

  • ✗ "Intake Metformin leads to GI upset."
  • ✓ "Taking Metformin may cause GI upset."
  • ✓ "Reduce your alcohol intake."

8-1. Emergency Action Guidance

SituationActionExample
Mild side effect (headache, cough, mild dizziness)contact your GP"Ramipril may cause a dry cough — contact your GP if this is troublesome."
Moderate side effect (bleeding, persistent vomiting)seek medical advice promptly"If vomiting lasts more than 24 hours, stop metformin and seek medical advice."
Emergency — patient can mobilise independentlygo to the emergency department"If chest pain develops, go to the emergency department immediately."
Emergency — patient cannot mobilise (stroke, anaphylaxis, heart attack)call 111 immediately"If sudden weakness, facial drooping or speech difficulty occurs, call 111 immediately."

⚠️ Why stroke recurrence requires "call 111" rather than "go to emergency department":

  • The patient may already be unable to move independently due to weakness/hemiplegia
  • Stroke symptoms (impaired consciousness, speech, movement) prevent the patient from judging or moving safely
  • Standard clinical guidance is for the carer to call emergency services immediately

10. During / While — Time Prepositions

❌ Incorrect: "During given this medication"

→ "During" is a preposition and can only be followed by a noun or gerund — not a past participle like "given".


ExpressionNuanceExample
While taking these medicationswhile currently taking them (default)While taking these medications, avoid alcohol.
Throughout your treatmentemphasises the whole periodThroughout your treatment, monitor for side effects.
During your treatmentduring the treatment periodDuring your treatment, it is important to...
During this course of treatmentduring this particular courseDuring this course of treatment, avoid grapefruit.
Whilst on these medicationswhile taking them (British English)Whilst on these medications, limit alcohol intake.
When taking [specific drug]when taking a specific drugWhen taking atorvastatin, avoid grapefruit.

11. Drug Name Capitalisation Rules

TypeDescriptionExamplesCapitalisation
Brand nameProprietary name given by manufacturerXarelto, Lipitor, Panadeine Forte, Trelegy ElliptaCapitalise first letter
Generic nameInternational active ingredient namerivaroxaban, atorvastatin, paracetamol, metforminLowercase

✅ Correct examples

  • "Xarelto (rivaroxaban) 15mg twice daily" — brand capitalised, generic lowercase
  • "take rivaroxaban 15mg twice daily" — lowercase when generic name used alone
  • "take Xarelto 15mg twice daily" — capitalised when brand name used alone

⚠️ Two practical rules

  • Be consistent — don't mix capitalised and lowercase forms of the same drug in one letter
  • Brand names must always be capitalised — "xarelto" in lowercase is incorrect

Recommended: introduce the drug once as "Xarelto (rivaroxaban)", then use one form consistently afterwards.

12. Five Techniques for Trimming Word Count

TechniqueBeforeAfter
1. Cut abstract sentences"Following the correct explanation, the usage of newly prescribed medication will be safe and effective."→ Delete entirely (no real information)
2. Combine info with a dash (—)"She takes one puff. It is taken once a day.""one puff, once daily"
3. Passive → active voice"GP appointment has been arranged""Your GP will see you in one week"
4. Merge repeated sentences"Like Doxycycline, Prednisolone course should be completed.""do not stop this medicine early" (merged into previous sentence)
5. Combine two drugs in one sentence"Furthermore, Ramipril was reduced. In addition, Clopidogrel was added.""Her ramipril was reduced to 2.5mg, and clopidogrel 75mg was started."

13. Genre & Style — Language by Reader

ReaderLanguage levelExample
GP/DoctorTechnical terminology acceptablehypoglycaemia, rhabdomyolysis, ACE inhibitor, eGFR
Carer (family member)Semi-technical — some terms with brief explanation"blood-thinning medication (anticoagulant)"
Patient (the patient themselves)Plain, everyday language"difficulty sleeping" (not insomnia), "upset stomach" (not GI upset)

Using technical jargon in a patient letter lowers the Genre & Style score — always replace it with plain language.

14. Ready-to-Use Phrases for OET Letters

Introductions

  • I am writing to advise you about your mother's medication following her discharge from hospital.
  • I am writing to outline the new medication prescribed for [patient name] and to raise some concerns regarding her current management.
  • I am writing to explain your new medicines following your recent hospital admission for [condition].

Explaining medication changes

  • This has been replaced by / switched to [drug] due to [reason].
  • The dose has been reduced to [X]mg daily for the same reason.
  • [Drug] has been prescribed to prevent further [condition].
  • Please complete the full course even if you start to feel better.
  • Do not stop this medication abruptly.

Side effects and warnings

  • Should [symptom] occur, please seek medical advice promptly.
  • If you notice any unusual bruising, blood in urine or stool, or severe headache, please contact her GP immediately.
  • If your breathing worsens, a fever develops, or there is no improvement within 48 hours, seek urgent medical attention.
  • If throat or lip swelling occurs, seek emergency help immediately.

Closing

  • Please do not hesitate to contact me if you have any further questions.
  • A follow-up appointment has been arranged with her GP in four weeks to recheck her renal function and blood pressure.
