1. Letter Layout

Based on official OET samples — always follow this order.

⚠️ Date goes after the address, before Dear — official OET sample order.

⚠️ DOB in the Re: line — include if given in case notes, omit if not.

2. The 6 OET Marking Criteria

CriterionWhat it assessesB-grade benchmark
PurposeIs the purpose of the letter clear?2+ out of 3
ContentIs the necessary clinical information accurate and complete?5+ out of 7
Conciseness & ClarityIs it written concisely, with only essential information?5+ out of 7
Genre & StyleIs the language appropriate for the reader?5+ out of 7
Organisation & LayoutIs the paragraph structure and letter format logical?5+ out of 7
LanguageIs the grammar, vocabulary, and spelling accurate?5+ out of 7

Key principle: if even one criterion is weak, the overall grade is pulled down — balance matters.

3. Word Count

  • Target range: 180–200 words (body only)
  • Date, address, greeting (Dear), and closing (Sincerely) are NOT counted
  • Exceeding the limit directly lowers the Conciseness & Clarity score
  • Main causes of overrunning: abstract introductions, overuse of linking words, repeated phrasing

4. Linking Words — Myth vs Fact

❌ Myth: using Furthermore / Moreover / In addition often raises your score.

✅ Fact: repeating the same linker reads like a memorised template and can lower your score.


FunctionLinking words you can use
Additionadditionally, furthermore, in addition, she also
Time/Sequenceon discharge, following this, subsequently, prior to, at the time of
Cause/Resultas a result, consequently, therefore, given that, due to
Contrasthowever, although, despite this, nevertheless
Specificationmore specifically, in particular, notably
Conditionshould [X] occur, if [X] develops, in the event of

Tip: don't use the same linker twice in one paragraph. Comma + "and" is often more concise.

5. Introduction — Common Mistake

❌ Weak — two content-free sentences wasting ~48 words

"Although you are already well aware of previous medication regimen, the instruction of current medication regimen is necessary to be checked. Following the correct explanation, the usage of newly prescribed medication will be safe and effective."


✅ Strong — purpose + clinical context in one sentence

"I am writing to explain your new medicines after your recent hospital stay for a COPD flare-up, including a change of inhaler and two new short courses of treatment."


Principle: longer introductions are fine when filled with real clinical information — not generic statements.

8-1. Emergency Action Guidance

SituationActionExample
Mild side effect (headache, cough, mild dizziness)contact your GP"Ramipril may cause a dry cough — contact your GP if this is troublesome."
Moderate side effect (bleeding, persistent vomiting)seek medical advice promptly"If vomiting lasts more than 24 hours, stop metformin and seek medical advice."
Emergency — patient can mobilisego to the emergency department"If chest pain develops, go to the emergency department immediately."
Emergency — patient cannot mobilise (stroke, anaphylaxis)call 111 immediately"If sudden weakness, facial drooping or speech difficulty occurs, call 111 immediately."

⚠️ Why stroke = "call 111" not "go to emergency department":

  • Patient may already be unable to move due to hemiplegia
  • Stroke symptoms prevent the patient from judging or moving safely
  • Standard clinical guidance: carer calls emergency services immediately

12. Five Techniques for Trimming Word Count

TechniqueBeforeAfter
1. Cut abstract sentences"Following the correct explanation, the usage of newly prescribed medication will be safe and effective."→ Delete entirely
2. Combine with a dash (—)"She takes one puff. It is taken once a day.""one puff, once daily"
3. Passive → active voice"GP appointment has been arranged""Your GP will see you in one week"
4. Merge repeated sentences"Like Doxycycline, Prednisolone course should be completed.""do not stop this medicine early" (merged)
5. Two drugs in one sentence"Furthermore, Ramipril was reduced. In addition, Clopidogrel was added.""Her ramipril was reduced to 2.5mg, and clopidogrel 75mg was started."

14. Ready-to-Use Phrases

Introductions

  • I am writing to advise you about your mother's medication following her discharge from hospital.
  • I am writing to outline the new medication prescribed for [patient name] and to raise some concerns regarding her current management.
  • I am writing to explain your new medicines following your recent hospital admission for [condition].

Explaining medication changes

  • This has been replaced by / switched to [drug] due to [reason].
  • The dose has been reduced to [X]mg daily for the same reason.
  • [Drug] has been prescribed to prevent further [condition].
  • Please complete the full course even if you start to feel better.
  • Do not stop this medication abruptly.

Side effects and warnings

  • Should [symptom] occur, please seek medical advice promptly.
  • If you notice any unusual bruising, blood in urine or stool, or severe headache, contact her GP immediately.
  • If your breathing worsens, a fever develops, or there is no improvement within 48 hours, seek urgent medical attention.
  • If throat or lip swelling occurs, seek emergency help immediately.

Closing

  • Please do not hesitate to contact me if you have any further questions.
  • A follow-up appointment has been arranged with her GP in four weeks to recheck her renal function and blood pressure.

⚠️ Recurring error pattern: adding "to" after verbs that already take a direct object.

Verb❌ Incorrect✅ Correct

Why this happens: Korean often uses a particle ("~에") that suggests a preposition is needed in English too — but these verbs already contain the meaning of "reaching/arriving at" something, so no preposition is required.

VerbCorrect example

Paragraph Structure — No Fixed Template

SituationBetter structureWhy
Each drug has independent side effectsHow to use → Side effects → AppointmentClean separation — reader can follow easily
Each drug has its own timing rules + closely linked side effectsDrug-by-drug (combine dosing + side effects per drug)Keeps related information together — avoids splitting one drug across two paragraphs
Heavy information load + word count pressureDrug-by-drugMore compact — saves words by avoiding repeated transitions

⚠️ OET does not require a fixed template. Organisation & Layout marks reward logical flow — not a specific paragraph order.

Key question to ask yourself: "If I split this information across two paragraphs, does the reader have to jump back and forth to understand one drug?" If yes — keep it together in one paragraph.


during vs whilst vs while — Full Explanation

WordPart of speechFollowed byExampleNotes
duringPrepositionNoun or noun phrase ONLYDuring the consultation... / During treatment...❌ Cannot be followed by a verb — "during taking" is wrong
whileConjunctionSubject + verb (full clause)While she was taking ramipril... / While taking this medication...✅ Can drop subject if same as main clause — "While taking" = "While she was taking"
whilstConjunctionSubject + verb (full clause)Whilst on these medications... / Whilst taking escitalopram...✅ British English equivalent of "while" — interchangeable in OET
❌ Incorrect✅ CorrectWhy
During taking escitalopramWhilst taking escitalopram / While taking escitalopram"during" cannot be followed by a gerund (taking)
During given this medicationDuring your treatment / Whilst on this medication"during" needs a noun — "given" is a past participle, not a noun
Whilst the consultationDuring the consultation / Whilst consulting"whilst" needs a verb — "the consultation" is a noun only
During she was taking ramiprilWhile/Whilst she was taking ramipril"during" cannot introduce a full clause with subject + verb

Simple rule to remember:

  • during + noun → "during the consultation / during treatment / during this course"
  • while/whilst + verb → "while taking / whilst on / while she was admitted"
  • while/whilst are interchangeable — both accepted in OET

"provide" — Correct Usage

StructureExampleJudgement
provide + objectWe provided booklets.✅ Natural
provide + person + with + thingI provided him with written instructions.✅ Natural — use for physical materials/resources
provide + person + with + adviceI provided him with medication advice.⚠️ Possible but "advised him on" is more natural
provide + that-clause❌ "He was provided that flare-ups are normal."❌ Never — "provide" cannot take a that-clause

Key rule: "provide" works best for tangible things (booklets, instructions, resources). For advice, education, and counselling — use the dedicated verbs above instead.
