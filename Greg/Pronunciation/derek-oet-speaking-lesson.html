Session 1 — role-play checklist

1 of 10 met. This is a baseline — expect steady gains as the items below are drilled.

Fix now Highest impact biggest, fastest gains

1. Cut the "ums"

Frequent filled hesitations. Hurts fluency and blurs structure. Silent pauses score better than "um".

Drill: when he feels an "um" coming, close the mouth and pause instead. Practise 1–2 second silent pauses between sentences until they feel normal.

2. Ask answerable questions

Questions are too broad ("what do you know about this medicine?") — the patient can't answer. Weakens information gathering.

Swap to narrow questions: "Has the doctor told you how often to take it?" • "Have you taken anything like this before?" • "Do you have any allergies?"

3. Signpost to add structure

No pauses means steps run together; the patient could get lost. Weakens providing structure.

Use structure phrases + a pause: "First, let me explain how to take it." • "Now, about the side effects..." • "So, to sum up..."

Build next Steady work longer payoff

4. Pronunciation: /s/ not /sh/

The s → sh substitution reduces intelligibility (a scored criterion, and his own priority).

Minimal pairs: sip / ship • sue / shoe • sea / she • save / shave • sock / shock

5. Pronunciation: /r/ not /l/

The r → l substitution also reduces intelligibility. Common and worth steady drilling.

Minimal pairs: right / light • rice / lice • road / load • correct / collect • pray / play

6. Show the patient's perspective

Currently a bit neutral. Cordial, but doesn't actively name the patient's feelings. Affects patient's perspective.

Empathy phrases: "I can see this is worrying you." • "That's a very good question." • "It's completely normal to feel unsure."

Polish later Already solid stretch goals

7. Widen grammar range

Grammar is accurate but sentences stay simple. OET rewards range, so a few complex structures lift the score.

Add: "If you miss a dose, you should..." • "The reason you need to finish the course is..." • "What I'd suggest is..."

8. Clarity when giving information

Already simple and jargon-free — just needs a bit more precision and a final check.

End with teach-back: "Just so I know it's clear, could you tell me how you'll take it?"

Strengths to keep: warm greeting & rapport, appropriate formality, accurate grammar, simple jargon-free language, growing confidence.

Whether it will work

"Will this cure me?"

✓"This medication treats it in most cases, but you'll still need a check-up with your doctor to be sure."

×"Yes, this will cure you." (false guarantee) — or a flat "I can't say" (no reassurance).

🔊"Dis med'cin treats-it in most-cases, but y'll still need-a check-up wid yer doctor ta be sure."

"How long until I feel better?"

✓"Most people start to feel better within a few days. If you don't notice any change after a few days, please go back to your doctor."

×An exact promise: "You'll be fine by Friday." Bodies vary — you can't guarantee it.

🔊"Most-people start-ta feel better widin-a few days. If-yu don' notice any change afta few days, please go-back ta yer doctor."

"Is this the strongest one you have?"

✓"Your doctor has chosen the dose that's right for you. Stronger isn't always better — this one is matched to your needs."

×"Yes, I'll give you something stronger." You can't change a prescribed dose — that's the prescriber's call.

🔊"Yer doctor's chosen the dose that's right-for-yu. Stronger isn't-always better — dis-wun's matched ta yer needs."

How to take it

"When should I take it?"

✓"It's best to take one tablet twice a day, with food — try to keep it to the same times each day."

×Vague: "Whenever, it's fine." Timing usually matters.

🔊"It's bes-t'take wun tablet twice-a-day, wid food — try-na keep-it ta the same-times each day."

"Do I take it with food or on an empty stomach?"

✓"This one's better with food — it helps reduce any stomach upset."

×Guessing. If you're unsure of a drug, check the label rather than invent an answer.

🔊"Dis-wun's better wid food — it helps reduce any stomach-upset."

"What if I forget a dose?"

✓"If you forget, take it as soon as you remember. But if it's almost time for the next one, just skip it — don't take two together."

×"Just take double next time." Dangerous — never advise doubling up.

🔊"If-yu forget, take-it az soon-az-yu r'member. But if-it's almos'-time for the next-wun, jus' skip-it — don' take two tagether."

"Can I stop when I feel better?" (antibiotics)

✓"It's really important to finish the whole course, even once you feel well. Stopping early can let the infection come back stronger."

×"Yes, that's fine once you feel okay." Wrong and unsafe for antibiotics.

🔊"It's really impor'nt ta finish the whole course, even-once-yu feel well. Stoppin' early can let the infection come-back stronger."

"Can I cut the tablet in half?"

✓"Some tablets can be split and some can't — let me check this one for you first."

×"Sure, that's fine." Some are slow-release and must not be split.

🔊"Sum tablets can-be split an' sum can't — leh'me check dis-wun for-yu first."

Side effects

"Will it have side effects?"

✓"You might notice some mild effects at first, such as a bit of nausea, and these usually settle. If anything feels severe, let me or your doctor know."

×"No, there are no side effects." Untrue for almost any drug — or listing every rare effect and scaring them.

🔊"Yu might notice sum mild effects-at furst, such-az a bit-a nausea, an' these usually settle. If anythin' feels severe, leh'me or yer doctor know."

"I've had a rash since starting it — is that normal?"

✓"Thank you for telling me. A rash can sometimes be a reaction, so I'd like you to stop it and speak to your doctor today, just to be safe."

×"Oh, that's nothing, keep taking it." A rash can signal an allergy — always take it seriously and refer.

🔊"Thank-yu for tellin' me. A rash can sometimes be-a reaction, so I'd like-yu ta stop-it an' speak ta yer doctor taday, jus' ta be safe."

Mixing with other things

"Can I drink alcohol with this?"

✓"It's best to avoid alcohol while you're on this medicine, as it can increase the side effects."

×"A little is fine." Depends on the drug — safer to advise avoiding, and check if unsure.

🔊"It's bes-t'avoid alca-hol while-y're on dis med'cin, az-it can increase the side-effects."

"Can I take it with my other tablets?"

✓"Let me check your other medicines for any interactions first — can you tell me everything you're taking, including anything bought without a prescription?"

×"That should be okay." Never assume — catching interactions is exactly the pharmacist's job.

🔊"Leh'me check yer other med'cins for any interactions first — can-yu tell-me everythin' y're takin', includin' anythin' bought widout a prescription?"

"I'm pregnant — is it safe?"

✓"That's an important question. Some medicines need extra care during pregnancy, so I'd like to check this one and confirm with your doctor before you take it."

×"It'll be fine." High-stakes — never reassure casually. Check and refer.

🔊"That's-an impor'nt question. Sum med'cins need extra-care durin' pregnancy, so I'd like-ta check dis-wun an' confirm wid yer doctor before-yu take-it."

Outside your scope — refer, don't answer

"What's wrong with me? Do you think I have an infection?"

✓"I'm not able to diagnose that, but from what you're describing it's best to see your doctor. In the meantime, I can help with the symptoms."

×"It sounds like you have [X]." Diagnosing is outside a pharmacist's role.

🔊"I'm not able-ta diagnose that, but from wha' y're describin' it's bes-t'see yer doctor. In the meantime, I can help wid the symptoms."

"Can you just give me something instead of the doctor?"

✓"For this, I'd really recommend seeing your doctor, because it needs a proper examination. I don't want to miss anything important."

×Handing over a prescription-only medicine, or talking them out of proper care.

🔊"For this, I'd really recommend seein' yer doctor, b'cause it needs-a proper examination. I don' wanna miss anythin' impor'nt."

"My friend has the same symptoms — can I give them my tablets?"

✓"It's best not to share medicine, even with the same symptoms — the cause can be different, and the dose is set for you. Your friend should come and see me."

×"Sure, that's fine." Never endorse sharing prescription medicine.

🔊"It's bes' not-ta share med'cin, even wid the same symptoms — the cause can-be different, an' the dose-is set for-yu. Yer friend should come-an' see me."

Cost

"Why is it so expensive? Is there a cheaper one?"

✓"There may be a generic version that works the same way for less — let me check for you. If cost is a real barrier, it's worth mentioning to your doctor too."

×Dismissing the concern, or promising a subsidy you can't confirm.

🔊"There may-be a generic version that works the same-way for less — leh'me check for-yu. If cost-is a real barrier, it's worth mentionin' ta yer doctor too."

Two OET reminders in every answer: give advice as suggestions ("it's best to...", "I'd recommend...") not orders; and nearly every safe answer either checks ("let me check for you") or refers ("please see your doctor") — that keeps Derek inside a pharmacist's scope.
