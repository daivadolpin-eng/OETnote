⏱ ~90 min 🗣 Focus: speaking 💊 Role: pharmacist 🎯 Skills: medication instructions & explaining treatment
For the tutor. You play the interlocutor (the patient or family member). Derek leads as the pharmacist. Give him the 3-minute prep timer with the card, then run the role-play for ~5 minutes. Stay in character — react as a real patient would, raise the concerns on your card, and let him lead. Save feedback for section 5.

How OET Speaking works

The whole sub-test takes about 20 minutes. Derek should know the shape of it before practising.

The structure

1. Warm-up (2 min, not scored): light chat about his job and experience, to reduce nerves.

2. Prep time (3 min per role-play): he reads the role card and makes notes. He may keep the card during the role-play.

3. Two role-plays (5 min each): he is always the health professional; the interlocutor is the patient or carer. He leads the conversation.

The 5 stages of a good role-play

1. Greet & build rapport — warm, professional opening.

2. Find out — ask what the patient knows / how they feel.

3. Explain / instruct — clear, simple, no jargon; advice as suggestions, not orders.

4. Check & respond — check understanding, deal with worries.

5. Close — summarise, next steps, invite questions.

Warm-up questions (run these first, ~2 min)

• Can you tell me a little about your work as a pharmacist?

• How long did you run pharmacies in Korea?

• What do you enjoy most about working with patients?

Role-play 1 — giving medication instructions

Focus skill: information giving. Give Derek the candidate card and start the 3-minute prep timer. Keep the interlocutor card for yourself.

3:00
Use "Prep" while Derek reads the card, then "Role-play" for the conversation.
Candidate card — give this to Derek

SETTING: A community pharmacy. A 58-year-old patient has just been prescribed metformin for newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes. This is their first time on the medication.

You are the pharmacist.

  • Greet the patient and build rapport.
  • Find out what they already understand about the medicine.
  • Explain clearly how and when to take metformin (one tablet twice a day, with meals).
  • Explain that mild stomach upset is common at first and usually settles.
  • Reassure the patient and check their understanding before they leave.
Interlocutor card (tutor only — don't show Derek)
Interlocutor card — the patient

You are a 58-year-old, recently told you have type 2 diabetes. You are a little anxious and not sure why you need medicine when you "feel fine."

  • Start slightly worried: "I don't really understand why I need this if I feel okay."
  • Ask: "Do I have to take it forever?"
  • Raise a concern: a friend told you it "upsets the stomach."
  • Only relax once the pharmacist explains clearly and reassures you.
  • Near the end, ask: "What if I forget a dose?"

Role-play 2 — explaining a treatment

Focus skill: explaining treatment & managing concern. Again: 3-minute prep, then ~5-minute role-play.

3:00
Use "Prep" while Derek reads the card, then "Role-play" for the conversation.
Candidate card — give this to Derek

SETTING: A community pharmacy. A 45-year-old patient collects a course of antibiotics (amoxicillin) for a chest infection. They have used antibiotics before and often stop early once they "feel better."

You are the pharmacist.

  • Greet the patient and check what the doctor has explained.
  • Explain how to take the course (three times a day, spaced out, for the full 7 days).
  • Explain clearly why they must finish the whole course, even if they feel better.
  • Explain, in simple terms, what antibiotic resistance is and why it matters.
  • Check understanding and answer their questions.
Interlocutor card (tutor only — don't show Derek)
Interlocutor card — the patient

You are 45, in a hurry, and think antibiotics are "no big deal." You usually stop taking them once you feel better.

  • Mention casually: "I usually stop these once I feel better — is that fine?"
  • Push back a little: "Seven days seems like a lot."
  • Ask: "What actually happens if I don't finish them?"
  • Only accept once the pharmacist explains resistance clearly and simply.
  • End by asking: "Can I drink alcohol with these?"

Assessment criteria & feedback

These are the nine official OET criteria. Use the checklist after each role-play to give Derek specific, targeted feedback — not just "good job."

Linguistic criteria (his stated weak area)

1

Intelligibility

Clear pronunciation, word stress, intonation. (Derek's main focus.)

2

Fluency

Smooth pace, natural pauses, few "ums" and false starts.

3

Appropriateness

Right level of formality; simple language for the patient.

4

Grammar & expression

Range and accuracy of structures and vocabulary.

Clinical communication criteria

5

Relationship-building

Warm greeting, empathy, patient-centred tone.

6

Patient's perspective

Acknowledges feelings, worries, and views.

7

Providing structure

Logical order; signposts each stage.

8

Information gathering

Good questions; checks what the patient knows.

9

Information giving

Clear, simple, jargon-free; advice as suggestions; checks understanding.

Quick feedback checklist

Tick what Derek did well; leave blank what to work on next time.

Intonation tip for instructions

When listing steps, use a rising tone on each item and a falling tone on the last one: "One tablet (rise), twice a day (rise), with food (fall)." This sounds clear and complete — and helps intelligibility.
